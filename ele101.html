This page gives you a simple, non-technical overview of how electricity works, how home electrical systems are organized, and what safety rules you should always follow. It’s meant to build understanding — not to replace a licensed electrician.

1. Safety Comes First — Always

Electricity is powerful and useful, but it can be extremely dangerous if handled incorrectly. Even low-voltage circuits or “just one wire” can be enough to shock, burn, or injure you.

Never do this

  • • Never touch bare wires or open panels.
  • • Never work on live circuits — power should be shut off at the breaker.
  • • Never use metal tools inside a live electrical box.
  • • Never try to “bypass” breakers, fuses, or safety devices.

Always do this

  • • Turn power off at the breaker before touching anything.
  • • Test that the power is truly off using a proper tester.
  • • Keep water away from outlets, cords, and tools.
  • • Call a licensed electrician for work you’re unsure about.

This page is educational only.

It is not a how-to guide for DIY wiring. Many electrical jobs require permits, inspections, and licensed professionals. When in doubt, treat electricity with respect and get help from a qualified electrician.

2. What Is Electricity?

At its simplest, electricity is the movement of tiny charged particles called electrons. When electrons flow through a material like a copper wire, we call that electric current.

The “water in a pipe” analogy

A helpful way to visualize electricity is to compare it to water flowing in pipes:

  • Voltage is like water pressure — it “pushes” the electrons.
  • Current is like the amount of water flowing.
  • Resistance is like the narrowing of the pipe — it resists the flow.

The more pressure (voltage) you have and the less resistance, the more current can flow — just like water.

Key units

  • Volt (V): unit of voltage
  • Ampere (A): unit of current
  • Ohm (Ω): unit of resistance
  • Watt (W): unit of power

In most homes in North America, standard outlets provide about 120 volts AC (alternating current).

3. Four Core Electrical Ideas

These four ideas show up again and again in electrical work. You’ll see them on labels, diagrams, and equipment.

Voltage (V)

The “push” behind the electrical flow — similar to pressure.

  • • Typical outlets: ~120 V
  • • Larger appliances: 240 V

Current (A)

The amount of electricity flowing through a wire.

  • • Higher current = more heat
  • • Wires and breakers are rated in amps

Resistance (Ω)

How much a material opposes current flow.

  • • Thin wires = higher resistance
  • • Heaters use high resistance to create heat

Power (W)

How much work is being done by electricity.

  • • Power = Voltage × Current
  • • Light bulbs are rated in watts

Ohm’s Law (for deeper understanding)

Ohm’s Law describes the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance:

V = I × R

Where V is voltage in volts, I is current in amps, and R is resistance in ohms.

4. Circuits & Your Home Electrical System

Electricity travels in circuits — closed loops that start at the panel, go through wires and devices, and return back to the panel.

Series vs. Parallel Circuits (Simple View)

Series circuit

Devices are lined up one after another in a single path.

  • • One path for current
  • • If one device fails, the whole path opens

Parallel circuit

Each device has its own path back to the source.

  • • Multiple paths for current
  • • One device can fail, others stay on

Home wiring is mostly parallel. That’s why one light bulb going out doesn’t turn off the rest of the house.

Main parts of a typical home system

  • Service drop / meter: Where power from the utility enters the home and is measured.
  • Main panel (breaker box): Distributes power to different circuits and provides protection.
  • Branch circuits: Wires that run to rooms, lights, outlets, and appliances.
  • Devices: Outlets, switches, lights, fans, appliances, etc.
  • Grounding system: Provides a safe path for fault current and helps reduce shock risk.

Breakers / Fuses

Protect wires from overheating if too much current flows. They trip or blow to stop the flow.

If a breaker keeps tripping, find the cause — don’t just reset it repeatedly.

GFCI

Ground-Fault Circuit Interrupter. Detects differences in current that may indicate a shock hazard and shuts off quickly.

Common in bathrooms, kitchens, garages, and outdoor outlets.

AFCI

Arc-Fault Circuit Interrupter. Detects dangerous arcing (tiny sparks) that can lead to fires.

Often required on bedroom and living area circuits in modern codes.

5. Everyday Electrical Safety Tips

Here are some simple habits that keep your home safer around electricity.

Good habits

  • • Unplug devices by the plug, not the cord.
  • • Replace damaged cords or cracked outlets.
  • • Keep cords away from heat sources and sharp edges.
  • • Use the correct wattage bulbs recommended for fixtures.
  • • Test GFCI outlets regularly using the “Test/Reset” buttons.

Warning signs

  • • Outlets or switches that feel warm or hot.
  • • Frequent breaker trips or flickering lights.
  • • Buzzing sounds from panels, outlets, or fixtures.
  • • Burning smell near electrical devices.
  • • Shocks or tingles when touching metal parts of appliances.

If you notice any of these, shut off power to that circuit and contact a licensed electrician.
