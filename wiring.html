Understanding Residential Wiring with RoboTechi

A simple, image-based overview of common wires, cables, and safety concepts you’ll find in homes. This is for learning only — not a DIY wiring manual.

Always turn power off and call a licensed electrician for real electrical work.

Big Picture: How Wiring Fits Together

Use these cards as a visual map: panel → cable → box → devices.

1. Main Panel

Power from the utility enters the main panel (breaker box), then splits into branch circuits that feed different areas of the home.

2. Cables in Walls

NM-B (“Romex”) and other cables carry hot, neutral, and ground conductors through framing cavities to rooms and devices.

3. Boxes & Devices

Junction boxes, outlets, switches, and fixtures make the wiring usable — and keep connections safe and accessible.

Common Wire & Cable Types (Visual)

These images represent the types of conductors and cables you’ll hear about in residential work.

THHN / THWN Conductors

Single insulated copper wires usually pulled through conduit. Common in panels, commercial work, and some residential runs.

  • • Each color is a separate conductor
  • • Needs conduit or raceway for protection

NM-B (“Romex”) Cable

A flat, plastic-jacketed cable with hot, neutral, and ground inside. Very common in modern homes.

  • • For dry, indoor use
  • • Not for direct burial or wet locations

UF (Underground Feeder) Cable

Tougher outer jacket for direct burial or wet locations like outdoor lighting or sheds.

  • • Rated for moisture and soil contact
  • • Often buried to a code-required depth

Reading Cable Markings (12/2, 14/3, etc.)

The print on the cable jacket tells you wire size, number of conductors, type, and rating. Knowing how to read it is key.

Example: 14/2 NM-B

  • • 14 = wire gauge (AWG)
  • • 2 = two insulated conductors inside (hot + neutral)
  • • Plus a bare ground wire
  • • Often used on 15A lighting or receptacle circuits

Example: 12/3 NM-B

  • • 12 gauge conductors
  • • 3 insulated wires (hot, hot, neutral) + ground
  • • Used for multi-wire branch circuits or 3-way switches
  • • Often on 20A circuits

Actual requirements depend on local electrical code and conditions. This is a simplified overview.

Typical Conductor Colors (North America)

  • Black / Red: Hot (live) conductors
  • White / Gray: Neutral conductors
  • Green / Bare: Equipment grounding conductors

Color conventions help identify each conductor’s role in the circuit and reduce dangerous mix-ups.

Why wire size matters

Wire that’s too small for the breaker can overheat before the breaker trips — a serious fire hazard. Matching gauge and breaker rating correctly is critical.

Wiring Knowledge vs. Doing the Work

Learning what you’re looking at — panels, cable types, wire sizes — is powerful. It helps you spot problems and talk clearly with electricians.

But actual wiring and rewiring should follow local code, permitting, and inspection rules.

Call a licensed electrician immediately if you see:

  • • Melted or charred insulation on wires
  • • Burning smells near panels, outlets, or fixtures
  • • Repeated tripped breakers with no clear cause
  • • Exposed splices outside of junction boxes
